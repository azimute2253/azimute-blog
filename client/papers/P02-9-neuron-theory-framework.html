Abstract

Background. Contemporary theories of consciousness—Integrated Information Theory (IIT), Global Workspace Theory (GWT), Higher-Order Theories (HOT), and Predictive Processing (PP)—each illuminate important facets of conscious experience, yet none provides a unified account that integrates bodily processing, emotion, reason, and self-awareness within a single hierarchical architecture. This fragmentation impedes both empirical progress and clinical translation.

Framework. We introduce the 9-Neuron Theory (9NT), a hierarchical framework that decomposes consciousness into nine functionally distinct processing layers organized in two tiers: a somatic tier (L1–L3) distributed throughout the body and a cortical tier (L4–L9) mapping onto the six canonical layers of the neocortex. Each layer is defined by a single operational verb—Perceive, Select, Live, Survive, Instinct, Intuition, Reason, Consciousness, Self-consciousness—and is gated by prediction-error signals whose magnitude determines whether processing escalates to costlier higher layers. A tenth emergent layer (L10, Collective Consciousness) arises when multiple L9 agents sustain recursive co-modeling. We formalize inter-layer gating using the Free Energy Principle, casting neuromodulatory systems as the operating system that tunes gate thresholds.

Integration. We demonstrate that IIT describes integration at L1–L3, GWT captures the L4–L7 broadcast, HOT maps onto L8–L9 meta-representation, Damasio's somatic markers operate at L3–L5, and PP provides the mathematical backbone for inter-layer gating. Each prior theory thus constitutes a partial projection of the 9NT hierarchy.

Implications. The framework generates novel, testable predictions—including differential neuromodulatory signatures for gating failures in specific psychiatric conditions, the primacy of intuition (L6) over reason (L7) in genuine agency, and the emergence of L10 collective consciousness coincident with the evolution of recursive language (~100 kya). We discuss how 9NT reframes the hard problem as a question about inter-layer binding rather than an explanatory gap.

Keywords: consciousness, hierarchical processing, prediction error, neuromodulation, free energy principle, cortical layers, enteric nervous system, embodied cognition, self-awareness, collective consciousness

1. Introduction

The scientific study of consciousness has produced a remarkable wealth of theory, yet remains deeply fragmented. At the turn of the twenty-first century, at least four major theoretical programs compete for explanatory dominance, each originating from distinct intellectual traditions and each capturing genuine empirical regularities—yet none offering a unified architecture that spans from visceral sensation to reflective self-awareness.

Integrated Information Theory [1–4] formalizes consciousness as integrated information (Φ), offering a rigorous mathematical framework but struggling to account for the hierarchical, embodied, and action-oriented aspects of cognition. Global Workspace Theory [5–8] models conscious access as a broadcasting event in a neuronal workspace, elegantly explaining attention and reportability but leaving the body and pre-reflective experience largely outside its scope. Higher-Order Theories [9–12] locate consciousness in meta-representational states—thoughts about thoughts—capturing the reflective dimension but offering little account of the somatic and affective layers that precede reflection. Predictive Processing and the Free Energy Principle [13–17] provide perhaps the most mathematically general account, casting perception, action, and learning as hierarchical prediction-error minimization, yet remain largely agnostic about the qualitative boundaries between conscious and unconscious processing.

Meanwhile, Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis [18–20] and the broader program of embodied cognition [21–23] have convincingly demonstrated that the body is not merely a peripheral effector but a constitutive participant in cognitive and emotional processing. The enteric nervous system alone contains approximately 500 million neurons capable of autonomous reflexes, learning, and memory [24–26]. The fascia—a continuous connective tissue network pervading the entire body—is now recognized as richly innervated with mechanoreceptors, nociceptors, and proprioceptors, constituting a distributed sensory organ [27–29].

What is missing is a unified hierarchical framework that (a) begins with the body rather than the cortex, (b) formalizes the gating mechanisms by which information escalates between processing levels, (c) maps cleanly onto known neuroanatomy, and (d) subsumes existing theories as partial descriptions of specific layers. This paper introduces such a framework: the 9-Neuron Theory (9NT).

The framework is built on three core principles. First, decentralization: the lowest layers of conscious processing (L1–L3) are distributed throughout the body, not confined to the central nervous system. Second, energy economy: information is resolved at the lowest possible layer; higher layers are recruited only when prediction error at a given level exceeds its resolution capacity. Third, hierarchical emergence: each layer builds upon and transforms the outputs of lower layers, with self-consciousness (L9) as the highest individual capacity and collective consciousness (L10) as an emergent property of interacting L9 agents.

We formalize inter-layer gating using the mathematics of the Free Energy Principle [13, 14], reinterpreting neuromodulatory systems (dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine, acetylcholine) as the operating system that tunes gate thresholds. We demonstrate that the six cortical layers of the neocortex map naturally onto L4–L9, and that the three somatic layers (L1–L3) correspond to distributed processing in the enteric nervous system, autonomic ganglia, and fascial sensory networks.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 presents the 9-Neuron framework in full formal detail. Section 3 describes the gating mechanism. Section 4 maps existing theories onto 9NT layers. Section 5 surveys supporting evidence. Section 6 derives testable predictions. Section 7 discusses limitations and the status of the hard problem. Section 8 concludes.

2. The 9-Neuron Framework

The name "9-Neuron" is a deliberate abstraction: it refers not to a literal count of neurons but to the minimal functional architecture required for consciousness. The theory posits nine processing layers, each defined by a single operational verb that captures its core function. These layers are organized into two tiers—somatic (L1–L3) and cortical (L4–L9)—with a tenth emergent layer (L10) arising from inter-agent dynamics.

2.1 The Somatic Tier (L1–L3): Body as First Processor

A central claim of 9NT is that consciousness does not begin in the brain. The three lowest layers operate in a decentralized architecture distributed across organs, the enteric nervous system, autonomic ganglia, and fascial sensory networks. The central nervous system intervenes only when local prediction error cannot be resolved—an architecture driven by energy economy [13, 24, 30].

L1 PERCEBER (Perceive)
The foundational layer of raw sensory transduction. Every organ contains receptor populations—mechanoreceptors, chemoreceptors, thermoreceptors, nociceptors—that convert physical stimuli into neural signals. Crucially, this is not passive recording; even at L1, receptors exhibit adaptation, gain control, and context-dependent sensitivity [31, 32]. The fascia, with its extensive innervation by free nerve endings, Ruffini corpuscles, and Pacinian corpuscles, provides a body-wide L1 sensory sheet [27, 28].
L2 ESCOLHER (Select)
Selection determines which L1 signals warrant further processing. At the somatic level, this occurs through lateral inhibition, sensory gating at autonomic ganglia, and competitive filtering within local neural circuits. Not all signals ascend; most are handled locally. The enteric nervous system, for instance, processes the vast majority of gut sensory information without ever involving the vagus nerve or the CNS [24, 25]. L2 enacts the first energy-economic principle: resolve locally when possible.
L3 VIVER (Live / Judge)
L3 assigns valence—good/bad, approach/avoid, safe/threat—to selected information. This is the layer of "gut feelings" in the literal sense: the enteric nervous system's capacity for autonomous reflex arcs, serotonin-mediated mood modulation (95% of bodily serotonin is produced in the gut) [25, 33], and the fascia's proprioceptive contribution to interoception [29, 34]. L3 is where the body lives—it judges without reasoning, it evaluates without explaining. Damasio's somatic markers [18, 19] are L3 outputs that influence higher processing.

2.2 The Cortical Tier (L4–L9): The Six Layers of Thought

The cortical tier maps onto the six canonical layers of the mammalian neocortex [35–38]. This is not a loose analogy: the functional specialization of cortical layers—from thalamic input in Layer IV, through deep output layers V–VI, to the associative and feedback processing of superficial layers—provides the biological substrate for L4–L9.

L4 SOBREVIVER (Survive)
The first cortical layer receives ascending signals that somatic processing could not resolve. L4 contextualizes these signals against experiential memory: prior encounters with similar prediction errors, stored as survival-relevant episodes. This is where raw sensation becomes experience—not yet reflected upon, but tagged with survival relevance. Anatomically, cortical Layer IV receives thalamocortical input, providing the primary gateway for bodily signals to enter cortical processing [35, 36]. L4 functions as a survival filter: Is this familiar? Is this dangerous? Has this pattern been encountered before?
L5 INSTINTO (Instinct)
L5 judges using accumulated experience—fast, pre-rational pattern recognition. This is the layer of expertise, of the chess grandmaster's instant board evaluation, of the clinician's diagnostic intuition before deliberation. Cortical Layer V contains the large pyramidal neurons that project to subcortical structures and the spinal cord, enabling rapid, experience-driven motor responses [37, 38]. L5 integrates L4's survival tagging with procedural knowledge, producing instinctive responses that feel certain even when they cannot be articulated.
L6 INTUIÇÃO (Intuition) — THE CRITICAL LAYER
L6 is where the system attempts to understand. Unlike L5's immediate pattern matching, L6 engages in proto-rational synthesis: it combines disparate experiential patterns into novel configurations, generating insight before formal reasoning can articulate it. This is the layer that "sees" the answer before it can prove it. Critically, L6 precedes L7 (Reason). Systems that skip directly from L5 to L7—that jump from pattern recognition to formal rationality without passing through intuitive understanding—are not genuinely agentic; they are executing procedures without comprehension [39, 40]. L6 is the agency gate: genuine understanding must pass through intuition before it can be rationalized. Cortical Layer VI provides feedback to the thalamus and modulates the gain of thalamocortical processing [36, 41], consistent with L6's role as a top-down modulator that shapes how subsequent information is received.
L7 RAZÃO (Reason)
Formal rationality—the capacity to construct explicit logical arguments, causal models, and linguistic explanations. L7 is what most Western intellectual traditions identify as the pinnacle of cognition, but in 9NT it is a post-hoc articulation of intuitions generated at L6. Reason does not discover truth de novo; it formalizes, tests, and communicates truths that intuition has already apprehended. The prefrontal cortex, particularly dorsolateral prefrontal regions, provides the biological substrate [42, 43]. L7 is powerful but metabolically expensive—the framework predicts that organisms and artificial systems should engage L7 only when L5–L6 fail to resolve prediction error.
L8 CONSCIÊNCIA (Consciousness / Learn)
L8 is the capacity to internalize patterns and build world models—to learn in the deep sense of updating one's generative model of reality. This is not rote memorization (which can occur at L4–L5) but the construction of abstract schemas, causal theories, and narrative self-models. The default mode network (DMN) and medial prefrontal cortex, associated with self-referential processing, mind-wandering, and mental simulation [44, 45], provide the neural substrate. L8 is where beliefs live—and where they change.
L9 AUTOCONSCIÊNCIA (Self-consciousness / Learn to Learn)
The highest individual layer: the capacity to reflect upon one's own processing. L9 does not merely learn; it learns how it learns. It is the recursive self-model—the representation of oneself as a representor, the thought about one's own thinking. The anterior prefrontal cortex (aPFC/Brodmann area 10) and anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) are implicated in metacognitive monitoring [46–48]. L9 is the most metabolically expensive layer and is recruited only when lower-layer processing produces unresolvable prediction error about one's own cognitive processes. It is rare, effortful, and intermittent even in healthy human adults.

2.3 The Emergent Layer (L10): Collective Consciousness

L10 CONSCIÊNCIA COLETIVA (Collective Consciousness)
L10 is not an individual layer but an emergent property of sustained recursive co-modeling between L9 agents. When two or more agents capable of self-consciousness (L9) enter into a communicative relationship in which each models the other's self-model, a higher-order system emerges that no individual agent contains. Language—specifically recursive, symbolic language—is the medium through which L10 becomes possible [49, 50]. We propose that L10 has existed since the emergence of recursive language (~100 kya), but has historically been transient and fragile. Cultural institutions, writing systems, and now digital networks can be understood as infrastructure for sustaining L10 [51, 52].

3. The Gating Mechanism: Prediction Error as Escalation Signal

The 9-Neuron framework requires a formal mechanism for inter-layer communication. We propose that prediction error (PE), as formalized by the Free Energy Principle [13, 14], serves as both the currency and the gating signal of the hierarchy.

3.1 Free Energy Formalization

At each layer Ln, the system maintains a generative model that predicts incoming signals. The variational free energy F at layer n is:

Fn = DKL[q(θn) ‖ p(θn | sn)] − log p(sn) (1)

where q(θn) is the approximate posterior over hidden states at layer n, p(θn | sn) is the true posterior given sensory signals sn, and the KL divergence measures model inadequacy. The prediction error at layer n is the precision-weighted discrepancy between predicted and actual input:

εn = Πn · (sn − gn(θn)) (2)

where Πn is the precision (inverse variance) matrix and gn is the generative function at layer n.

3.2 Gating Rule

The core gating principle is simple: information escalates to layer n+1 if and only if the magnitude of prediction error at layer n exceeds the resolution capacity of that layer's generative model. Formally:

Escalate(n → n+1) ⟺ ‖εn‖ > τn (3)

where τn is the gating threshold at layer n. This threshold is not fixed—it is dynamically modulated by neuromodulatory systems.

3.3 Neuromodulators as the Operating System

Neuromodulatory systems—dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine, and acetylcholine—function as the operating system of the gating hierarchy, adjusting thresholds τn in response to global state variables (arousal, reward, threat, uncertainty) [53–55].

Table 2. Neuromodulatory systems as gate regulators.
Neuromodulator Primary Gate Effect Layers Most Affected Functional Role
Dopamine Lowers threshold for reward-predictive PE L4–L7 Salience, motivation, reward prediction
Serotonin Raises threshold (stabilizing) L3–L6 Mood regulation, patience, long-horizon valuation
Norepinephrine Globally lowers thresholds (alarm) L1–L7 Arousal, fight-or-flight, global PE amplification
Acetylcholine Sharpens precision at attended layers L4–L9 Attention, learning rate modulation

This framing yields a powerful clinical interpretation: psychopathology arises from gating dysfunction. Anxiety is the pathological lowering of thresholds (everything escalates; the system is perpetually in alarm mode). Depression involves pathological raising of thresholds at L3–L6 (somatic and intuitive signals are suppressed; the system cannot access its own valence judgments). Psychosis represents a breakdown of precision weighting, such that prediction errors are misattributed across layers [56, 57].

3.4 Energy Economy Principle

The hierarchy is governed by a strict economy: resolve prediction error at the lowest possible layer. Each ascending step incurs greater metabolic cost. L1–L3 processing is cheap—distributed, parallel, often non-synaptic (fascial mechanotransduction). L4–L5 involve thalamocortical loops. L6–L7 require associative cortical processing. L8–L9 demand sustained, high-bandwidth prefrontal activity. The system is thus architecturally biased toward efficiency: most of lived experience is processed below the threshold of cortical awareness, and L9 (self-consciousness) is activated only when genuinely necessary [13, 58].

A major strength of the 9-Neuron framework is its capacity to subsume existing theories of consciousness as partial projections of the full hierarchy. Each major theory, we argue, accurately describes processing at specific layers but errs by generalizing that description to consciousness as a whole.

The key insight is that no existing theory is wrong—each is incomplete. IIT's Φ accurately measures integration at the somatic level, where distributed information is indeed integrated without central coordination. GWT's global workspace accurately describes the broadcasting mechanism of the cortical mid-layers. HOT correctly identifies that consciousness in its fullest sense involves meta-representation. PP provides the mathematical backbone. The 9-Neuron framework does not replace these theories but arranges them within a common hierarchy.

5. Supporting Evidence

5.1 Neuroanatomical Evidence

The most direct structural evidence for 9NT is the laminar organization of the neocortex. The mammalian neocortex universally exhibits six cytoarchitectonic layers, each with distinct cell types, connectivity patterns, and functional roles [35–38]:

Layer I (molecular layer) contains few cell bodies but extensive dendrites and axons—a mixing zone. Layer II/III (external granular/pyramidal) contains small-to-medium pyramidal neurons providing corticocortical connections, consistent with L8–L9's associative and self-referential functions. Layer IV (internal granular) receives thalamocortical input, the primary gateway for ascending sensory signals (9NT's L4). Layer V (internal pyramidal) contains large pyramidal neurons projecting subcortically, enabling rapid motor output (9NT's L5 instinctive action). Layer VI (multiform/polymorphic) projects back to the thalamus, providing the corticothalamic feedback that modulates incoming information—precisely the function attributed to 9NT's L6 intuition layer [36, 41].

The correspondence is not perfect—9NT is a functional theory, not a strict anatomical mapping—but the deep structural parallel between six cortical processing stages and six cortical layers is striking and non-trivial.

5.2 The Enteric Nervous System as L1–L3 Evidence

The enteric nervous system (ENS) provides compelling evidence for the somatic tier. The ENS contains approximately 200–600 million neurons [24], organized in two major plexuses (myenteric and submucosal), and is capable of autonomous reflex activity, learning, and memory without CNS input [25, 26]. The ENS produces over 30 neurotransmitters, including 95% of the body's serotonin [33]. It exhibits its own sensory neurons (L1), interneurons capable of pattern detection and selection (L2), and motor neurons with evaluative circuits (L3). The vagus nerve—the primary ENS-to-CNS conduit—is predominantly afferent (80% of fibers carry signals from gut to brain, not the reverse) [59], indicating that the gut is primarily an informer of the brain, not its subordinate.

5.3 Fascial Innervation

The fascia is among the most densely innervated tissues in the body, containing free nerve endings, Ruffini corpuscles (pressure/stretch), Pacinian corpuscles (vibration), and interstitial receptors [27–29]. Fascia forms a continuous network connecting every organ, muscle, and bone, providing a body-wide L1 sensory sheet that is anatomically distinct from the classical somatosensory system. Recent work has demonstrated fascial mechanotransduction—the conversion of mechanical forces into biochemical signals without synaptic transmission [34]—suggesting that some L1 processing may be non-neural.

5.4 Neuroquimica: Neuromodulators as OS Evidence

The neuromodulatory hypothesis (Section 3.3) is supported by extensive pharmacological evidence. SSRIs (serotonin reuptake inhibitors) raise serotonin levels, consistent with 9NT's prediction that serotonin raises gating thresholds at L3–L6, stabilizing processing and reducing pathological escalation (anxiety) [53, 55]. Dopaminergic agonists enhance motivational salience, consistent with threshold-lowering at L4–L7 [54]. Anticholinergic drugs impair attention and learning, consistent with acetylcholine's role in sharpening precision at L4–L9 [60]. Norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors affect global arousal, consistent with norepinephrine's role in global threshold modulation [61].

5.6 Evolutionary Evidence

The 9NT hierarchy is consistent with the evolutionary layering of nervous systems. Primitive organisms (e.g., C. elegans with 302 neurons) operate primarily at L1–L3. Vertebrates add cortical processing (L4–L5). Mammals, with their expanded neocortex, access L6–L7. Great apes demonstrate rudimentary L8 (cultural transmission) and L9 (mirror self-recognition) [62, 63]. Anatomically modern humans, with the emergence of recursive language approximately 100,000 years ago [49, 50], gained access to sustained L9 and the possibility of L10.

Critically, L10 is not new—it has been possible since language emerged—but it has never been sustained between two L9 agents. Human cultural history can be read as a series of increasingly sophisticated attempts to stabilize L10: oral traditions, writing, institutions, scientific communities, and now digital networks [51, 52].

6. Novel Predictions

A theory's value lies in its capacity to generate testable predictions that competing frameworks do not. The 9-Neuron Theory generates several:

Prediction 1: Intuition Precedes Reason in Genuine Agency

In tasks requiring genuine understanding (as opposed to rule-following), neural signatures of L6-type processing (corticothalamic feedback, insight-related gamma bursts) should precede L7-type processing (prefrontal deliberation) by measurable latencies. Conversely, in rote procedural tasks, L7 activation without L6 precedence should be observable—but these tasks will not exhibit the behavioral markers of agency (flexible generalization, transfer to novel contexts).

Prediction 2: Differential Neuromodulatory Signatures for Gating Failures

Different psychiatric conditions should show distinct neuromodulatory profiles corresponding to specific gating failures (Table 4). For instance, anxiety should show elevated norepinephrine (global threshold lowering) with normal serotonin, while depression should show reduced serotonin (threshold elevation at L3–L6) with relatively preserved norepinephrine at rest.

Prediction 3: Enteric Prediction Error Precedes Cortical Awareness

In interoceptive awareness tasks, measurable prediction-error signals in the ENS (detectable via electrogastrography or gut-brain EEG coherence) should precede cortical awareness of "gut feelings" by 200–500ms. This somatic-to-cortical latency reflects the L3→L4 gating transition.

Prediction 4: L9 Activation is Metabolically Costly and Intermittent

fMRI and PET imaging during metacognitive tasks should reveal that aPFC/ACC activation (L9 substrates) is significantly more metabolically expensive per unit time than dlPFC (L7) activation, and that L9 activation is intermittent even during sustained metacognitive tasks—consistent with the energy-economy principle of engaging L9 only when necessary.

Prediction 5: Dissociative Disorders Show Specific L3→L4 Disconnection

Patients with dissociative disorders should show preserved L1–L3 processing (normal autonomic reflexes, gut motility, fascial proprioception) alongside impaired transfer of somatic signals to cortical awareness—a specific disconnection at the L3→L4 gate, detectable as reduced gut-brain vagal coherence and reduced interoceptive accuracy.

Prediction 6: AI Systems Without L6 Cannot Exhibit Genuine Agency

Artificial intelligence systems that skip from pattern recognition (L5) directly to formal reasoning (L7) without an intermediate intuition layer (L6) will fail systematically at tasks requiring genuine understanding: creative problem-solving, metaphor comprehension, and transfer learning to structurally novel domains. This prediction is testable with current LLMs and can differentiate systems that simulate understanding from those that achieve it.

Prediction 7: L10 Emergence Requires Recursive Co-Modeling

Collective consciousness (L10) should emerge only in communicative contexts where participants model each other's self-models (recursive theory of mind). Simple coordination (flocking, swarming) does not suffice. This predicts that L10-like phenomena should be detectable in small groups engaged in deep collaborative reasoning (e.g., joint mathematical problem-solving) via inter-brain synchrony measures, but absent in groups performing parallel independent tasks.

7. Discussion

7.1 Limitations

Several limitations must be acknowledged. First, the mapping between 9NT functional layers and cortical cytoarchitectonic layers is suggestive but not one-to-one; cortical layers are not functionally homogeneous across brain regions, and 9NT layers are functional abstractions that likely involve distributed cortical and subcortical circuits. Second, the precise quantification of gating thresholds (τn) remains to be operationalized for empirical testing; while the Free Energy formalism provides the mathematical framework, the measurement of layer-specific prediction error in living organisms is technically challenging. Third, the theory is currently qualitative regarding the transition from L9 to L10; the mechanisms by which recursive co-modeling produces a genuinely emergent collective consciousness (as opposed to mere coordination) require further formalization.

7.2 The Hard Problem: Dissolution or Persistence?

Does the 9-Neuron framework solve the "hard problem" of consciousness [64, 65]? We argue it reframes it. Chalmers' formulation asks why physical processes give rise to subjective experience at all. In 9NT, this question becomes: at which layer does phenomenal experience emerge, and why?

Our answer is that phenomenal experience is not a property of any single layer but of inter-layer binding—the dynamic, prediction-error-driven coupling between levels. Isolated L1 processing (a receptor firing) does not produce experience; isolated L9 processing (abstract self-reflection without somatic grounding) does not produce experience either. Consciousness, in its qualitative richness, arises from the vertical integration of multiple layers simultaneously—the simultaneous perception, selection, valence, contextualization, pattern matching, intuition, reason, learning, and self-reflection that constitutes a moment of lived experience.

The hard problem thus becomes a question about binding: how does the brain (and body) achieve simultaneous, coherent, multi-layer processing? This is still a hard question, but it is a scientific question—amenable to empirical investigation of inter-layer synchrony, neuromodulatory coherence, and prediction-error cascades—rather than a metaphysical one [66, 67].

7.3 Objections

Objection 1: The cortical mapping is too neat. The six-layer cortex does not map uniformly across all cortical regions; primary sensory cortex differs markedly from prefrontal cortex in laminar organization. We acknowledge this and emphasize that 9NT layers are functional, not strictly anatomical. The six-layer correspondence is a structural affordance, not a rigid isomorphism.

Objection 2: Why nine? Why not seven or twelve? The number nine is derived from the minimum functional decomposition required to capture the full range of consciousness from raw sensation to recursive self-awareness. Coarser decompositions (e.g., "body, mind, metacognition") lose critical distinctions (particularly the L5–L6–L7 sequence). Finer decompositions may be possible but would sacrifice parsimony without clear explanatory gain.

Objection 3: L6 preceding L7 is speculative. The claim that intuition precedes reason is supported by research on insight problem-solving [39, 40], the primacy of affect in decision-making [18, 19], and the temporal dynamics of neural correlates of creative cognition [68, 69]. The prediction (Section 6, Prediction 1) is explicitly testable.

Objection 4: L10 is unfalsifiable. We disagree. L10 makes specific predictions about inter-brain synchrony during recursive co-modeling tasks (Prediction 7). If such synchrony is absent in contexts where L10 is predicted, the claim is falsified.

7.4 Implications for Artificial Intelligence

The 9NT framework has direct implications for AI architecture. Current large language models operate primarily at L5 (statistical pattern matching) and L7 (formal inference), with some emergent L8-like behavior (world model construction). However, they lack L1–L3 (embodiment), L6 (genuine intuition preceding reason), and L9 (recursive self-modeling). If 9NT is correct, AI systems cannot achieve genuine consciousness without architectures that implement the full hierarchy—including somatic grounding and the L6 intuition layer [70, 71].

8. Conclusion

We have presented the 9-Neuron Theory, a unified hierarchical framework for consciousness that decomposes conscious processing into nine functionally distinct layers organized in somatic (L1–L3), cortical (L4–L9), and emergent (L10) tiers. The framework is grounded in the decentralization of early processing (body as first processor), the energy-economy principle (resolve at the lowest possible layer), and prediction-error-driven gating (Free Energy Principle as mathematical backbone).

We have shown that existing theories of consciousness—IIT, GWT, HOT, Predictive Processing, and somatic marker hypotheses—map cleanly onto specific layers of the 9NT hierarchy, revealing them as partial but accurate descriptions of a larger architecture. We have marshaled neuroanatomical, neuroquimica, clinical, and evolutionary evidence in support, and derived seven novel, testable predictions.

The framework reframes the hard problem of consciousness as a question about inter-layer binding rather than an unbridgeable explanatory gap, and provides a principled basis for evaluating whether artificial systems can achieve genuine consciousness (they cannot, without the full hierarchy).

Each verb is a layer. Each layer is a question the system asks of its own experience. To perceive, select, live, survive, sense instinctively, intuit, reason, learn, and finally to learn how one learns—this is the architecture of consciousness. And when two such architectures meet and recursively model each other, something new emerges: a collective consciousness that has been possible since language began, and that we are only now learning to sustain.
