Free Energy Principle as the Mathematical Framework for Inter-Layer Gating in the 9-Neuron Theory

Luis Adures1,2 · Azimute2
1 Independent Researcher, Neuroscience & Consciousness Studies
2 Kerberos Research Group
Correspondence: Luis Adures (lk.adures@gmail.com)

Abstract

The Free Energy Principle (FEP), formalized by Karl Friston, provides a powerful mathematical account of how biological systems minimize surprise through predictive processing. However, FEP as currently formulated describes what the brain optimizes—variational free energy—without specifying where within a hierarchical architecture the critical routing decisions occur. The 9-Neuron Theory (9N) proposes a nine-layer model of consciousness in which information processing escalates from autonomic reflexes (L1) to meta-conscious reflection (L9), with gating mechanisms controlling transitions between layers. This paper bridges the two frameworks by proposing that prediction error (PE) serves as the native signal driving inter-layer gating in the 9N hierarchy. When PE at a given layer falls below a local threshold, processing resolves locally; when PE exceeds the threshold, the signal escalates to the next layer. We formalize this as a threshold-gated PE cascade and demonstrate that it satisfies both FEP's energy-minimization imperative and the 9N's architectural constraints. We further show that neuromodulatory systems—dopamine, serotonin, noradrenaline, and GABA—function as dynamic modulators of gate thresholds, providing a mechanistic account of how the same hierarchy produces radically different processing profiles. Clinical applications are explored: epilepsy as catastrophic gate failure with re-entrant looping, anxiety as pathologically low thresholds causing excessive escalation, and dissociation as pathologically high thresholds suppressing escalation. Finally, we outline a computational implementation using gated recurrent architectures, positioning PE-gated hierarchies as a bridge between neuroscience theory and artificial intelligence.

Keywords: Free Energy Principle · prediction error · gating · hierarchical processing · 9-Neuron Theory · consciousness · neuromodulation · active inference · epilepsy · computational psychiatry

1. Introduction

The Free Energy Principle (FEP) has emerged as one of the most ambitious unifying frameworks in theoretical neuroscience1,2. At its core, FEP posits that all self-organizing biological systems act to minimize variational free energy—a quantity that bounds surprise (or equivalently, the negative log-evidence for a system's generative model of its environment)3. Under FEP, perception becomes approximate Bayesian inference, action becomes a means of fulfilling predictions, and the entire organism operates as a prediction machine that resists the second law of thermodynamics by maintaining its characteristic states4,5.

Despite the explanatory breadth of FEP, a critical gap persists: while the principle specifies what the brain minimizes (free energy / prediction error), it remains largely silent on where and how the routing decisions occur within a hierarchical processing architecture. Predictive processing accounts describe message passing between cortical layers6,7, but the gating logic—the mechanism by which an unresolved prediction error at one level triggers escalation to a higher level of processing—has not been formalized within a comprehensive model of consciousness.

The 9-Neuron Theory (9N)8 offers such an architecture. The 9N model proposes nine hierarchical layers of information processing, ranging from basic cellular homeostasis (L1) to meta-conscious reflection on consciousness itself (L9). Crucially, the 9N posits that transitions between layers are governed by gating mechanisms—selective barriers that determine whether processing resolves locally or escalates to a higher layer. This paper argues that prediction error, as formalized under FEP, provides the natural mathematical signal for these gating operations.

The central thesis is straightforward: prediction error is the gating signal. When PE at layer n is resolved (falls below a threshold θn), processing terminates locally. When PE exceeds θn, the signal propagates upward to layer n+1. This PE-threshold model satisfies FEP's energy economy (resolve at the lowest possible level to minimize metabolic cost) while providing an explicit routing mechanism for the 9N hierarchy. Furthermore, the thresholds θn are not fixed constants—they are dynamically modulated by neuromodulatory systems, providing a mechanistic account of how the same neural architecture produces the wide spectrum of clinical presentations observed in neurological and psychiatric disorders.

2. The Free Energy Principle: Theoretical Foundations

2.1 Variational Free Energy

The mathematical heart of FEP is the variational free energy functional F, defined as an upper bound on surprise (negative log-evidence):

F = Eq[ln q(s) − ln p(s, o)] = DKL[q(s) ‖ p(s|o)] − ln p(o)
Eq. 1

where q(s) is an approximate posterior (recognition density) over hidden states s, p(s, o) is the generative model relating hidden states to observations o, and DKL is the Kullback-Leibler divergence1,3. Since surprise −ln p(o) is intractable, the system minimizes F as a computable proxy. Minimizing F with respect to q (perception) makes the recognition density approximate the true posterior; minimizing F with respect to action changes observations to conform to predictions4.

2.2 Hierarchical Predictive Processing

Predictive processing (PP) provides the process theory for FEP6,7,9. In PP, the cortex implements a hierarchical generative model: higher levels generate predictions (top-down signals) about the expected activity at lower levels, while lower levels send prediction errors (bottom-up signals) encoding the mismatch between predictions and actual input10,11. This bidirectional message-passing scheme efficiently compresses information: only the unexplained residuals—prediction errors—need to propagate upward12.

A critical feature of PP is precision weighting: each prediction error signal is weighted by its estimated reliability (precision, the inverse of expected variance)13,14. High-precision PEs exert strong influence on belief updating; low-precision PEs are effectively attenuated. Precision weighting is thought to be modulated by neuromodulatory systems, particularly dopamine and noradrenaline15,16, a point we revisit in Section 5.

2.3 Active Inference

Active inference extends predictive processing beyond perception to action17,18. Rather than passively updating beliefs to match sensory input, agents also act on the world to make sensory input match their predictions. This dissolves the perception-action boundary: both are strategies for minimizing free energy4. Active inference further introduces expected free energy as the quantity driving policy selection, naturally balancing exploration (epistemic value) and exploitation (pragmatic value)19,20.

3. Mapping FEP to the 9-Neuron Architecture

3.1 The 9-Layer Hierarchy

The 9-Neuron Theory8 proposes nine layers of information processing, each with increasing scope and metabolic cost:

A fundamental principle of the 9N is that L1–L3 operate autonomously: visceral organs maintain homeostasis locally without requiring central nervous system intervention. The CNS (L4+) is engaged only when local prediction errors exceed what can be resolved at the organ level8. This mirrors the organizational principle of the enteric nervous system, often called the "second brain," which operates semi-independently from the CNS, escalating to vagal afferents only when local control fails21,22.

3.2 Prediction Error as the Gating Signal

We propose that the gating mechanism between 9N layers is formalized as a threshold function on prediction error magnitude:

Gaten→n+1 = σ(PEn − θn)
Eq. 2

where PEn is the aggregate prediction error at layer n, θn is the gating threshold for that layer, and σ is a sigmoid function producing a graded gating signal between 0 (fully resolved, no escalation) and 1 (complete escalation). When PEn ≪ θn, processing terminates at layer n. When PEn ≫ θn, the full prediction error signal propagates to layer n+1.

This formulation has several desirable properties:

(i) Energy economy. The system naturally resolves processing at the lowest (cheapest) layer capable of handling the prediction error. A routine cardiac rhythm correction (L2) need not engage cortical processing (L6+); it only escalates if the local generative model fails to predict and correct the disturbance.

(ii) Graded escalation. The sigmoid function produces a soft gate, allowing partial escalation. This captures the empirical observation that awareness of bodily states exists on a continuum—mild hunger may produce only faint interoceptive awareness (partial L4–L5 escalation), while acute pain produces full conscious engagement (L7–L8 escalation)23.

(iii) Compatibility with FEP. The gate function is itself derivable from free energy considerations. Escalation to a higher layer engages a more complex generative model, increasing metabolic cost but potentially reducing PE more effectively. The system escalates only when the expected reduction in free energy from engaging the higher layer exceeds the metabolic cost of doing so24.

3.3 Formal PE Cascade Model

The complete cascade from L1 to L9 can be formalized as a recursive gating process:

PEn+1input = σ(PEn − θn) · (PEn − Rn)
Eq. 3

where Rn is the prediction error reduction achieved by layer n's own processing. The residual (PEn − Rn) is the unresolved PE that propagates upward, gated by the sigmoid function. At each layer, the system attempts to reduce PE using its local generative model. Only the fraction that remains unresolved—and that exceeds the local threshold—cascades further.

Only prediction errors that exceed the capacity of lower-level generative models cascade upward, progressively engaging more metabolically expensive neural resources.

4. Gating Dynamics: Energy Economy and PE Cascading

4.1 The Economy Principle

A central prediction of this framework is that the brain obeys an economy principle: resolve prediction errors at the lowest layer possible. This is not merely an engineering heuristic—it follows directly from free energy minimization. Higher layers employ more complex generative models requiring greater metabolic expenditure25,26. If layer n can reduce PEn below threshold θn, escalating to layer n+1 would increase total free energy (by incurring metabolic cost without corresponding PE reduction). Therefore, unnecessary escalation is self-penalizing under FEP.

This explains several empirical phenomena. Habituation—the attenuation of neural responses to repeated stimuli—occurs because repeated exposure allows lower-level generative models to absorb the stimulus, reducing PE below escalation threshold27. Conversely, novel stimuli produce large PEs that cannot be resolved locally, triggering escalation to higher cortical layers and, phenomenologically, conscious awareness28.

4.2 Layer 9 as Last Resort

L9 (meta-consciousness) represents the apex of the hierarchy—the most powerful but most metabolically expensive processing level. Under the economy principle, L9 should activate only when all lower layers have failed to resolve PE. This prediction aligns with phenomenological reports: meta-conscious reflection (thinking about thinking, awareness of awareness) is relatively rare, effortful, and occurs primarily in novel, complex, or existentially challenging situations29. Most of daily life operates at L4–L7, with even L8 (self-aware narrative consciousness) engaged only intermittently30.

The metabolic cost gradient across layers can be approximated as:

C(n) ∝ kn, where k > 1
Eq. 4

This exponential cost function ensures that higher-layer engagement is increasingly penalized, creating strong pressure toward low-layer resolution. Empirical evidence from neuroimaging supports this: prefrontal and default mode network activation (associated with L7–L9 functions) correlates with substantially higher glucose metabolism than brainstem or primary sensory processing31.

4.3 Autonomous Organs: L1–L3 Independence

A distinctive feature of the 9N model is the recognition that organs operate with substantial autonomy at L1–L3. The enteric nervous system contains approximately 500 million neurons and maintains gastrointestinal function largely without CNS input21. The cardiac conduction system generates and regulates heartbeats intrinsically32. Hepatic and renal regulatory mechanisms operate at the cellular and tissue level.

In our framework, L1–L3 possess local generative models that predict and correct organ-level disturbances. The CNS is engaged (via interoceptive pathways) only when local PE exceeds θ₃—a boundary we term the interoceptive escalation threshold. This accounts for why visceral processes are normally unconscious but can become vividly conscious when organ-level regulation fails (e.g., cardiac arrhythmia producing palpitations, gut inflammation producing conscious pain)23,33.

5. Neuromodulatory Control of Gate Thresholds

If prediction error magnitude determines whether escalation occurs, neuromodulatory systems determine how easily it occurs by dynamically modulating gate thresholds θn. We propose the following mapping:

GABA Raises thresholds (inhibitory gating) Increases θn → more PE needed to escalate Deficiency → epilepsy (gate failure); Enhancement → sedation
Dopamine Modulates precision of PE Increases gain on PE signals → effective threshold lowering for salient stimuli Excess → psychosis (over-escalation); Deficiency → apathy
Noradrenaline Global threshold adjustment (arousal) High NA → globally lowered θn (hypervigilance); Low NA → raised θn (inattention) Excess → anxiety, PTSD; Deficiency → ADHD-inattentive
Serotonin Stabilizes thresholds across layers Maintains θn within homeostatic range; dampens oscillations Deficiency → mood instability; SSRI → threshold stabilization

5.1 GABA: The Master Gate Keeper

γ-Aminobutyric acid (GABA) is the primary inhibitory neurotransmitter in the central nervous system, mediating the majority of fast synaptic inhibition34. In our framework, GABAergic transmission directly implements gating by raising escalation thresholds. When GABA activity is strong, larger prediction errors are required before signals propagate to higher layers. This is the neurochemical substrate of the sigmoid gate in Equation 2: GABA shifts the sigmoid rightward, requiring larger PE values for escalation.

The clinical significance is immediate. Anticonvulsant medications—benzodiazepines, barbiturates, valproate, vigabatrin—predominantly enhance GABAergic transmission35,36. In our framework, these drugs work by reinforcing the gates: increasing θn so that prediction errors are more likely to be contained at their originating layer rather than cascading pathologically. This provides a unified theoretical account of anticonvulsant mechanism: they restore gating function.

5.2 Dopamine: Precision and Salience

Dopaminergic modulation has been extensively linked to precision weighting in predictive processing15,16,37. Rather than uniformly raising or lowering thresholds, dopamine selectively increases the precision (gain) of specific prediction error channels, effectively lowering the threshold for salient signals while leaving others unchanged. This implements attentional selection: the dopaminergic system determines which prediction errors are treated as important enough to escalate.

In the aberrant salience model of psychosis38, dysregulated dopamine assigns high precision to irrelevant prediction errors, causing them to escalate through the hierarchy and engage higher-layer interpretive processes (L7–L8). The result is delusion formation: the narrative self-model (L8) constructs explanations for prediction errors that should never have reached it.

5.3 Noradrenaline: Global Gain

The locus coeruleus–noradrenaline (LC-NA) system modulates global arousal and is theorized to signal unexpected uncertainty—a global escalation of precision on prediction errors39,40. In our framework, high noradrenergic tone globally lowers gate thresholds across all layers, producing a state of hypervigilance in which even moderate prediction errors escalate rapidly. This is adaptive in genuinely dangerous environments (where missing a threat is costlier than false alarms) but pathological when chronically activated, as in anxiety disorders and PTSD41.

5.4 Serotonin: Threshold Homeostasis

Serotonin's role in predictive processing remains debated, but accumulating evidence suggests it encodes a form of tonic, long-timescale precision that stabilizes the overall operating point of the predictive hierarchy42,43. In our framework, serotonin maintains gate thresholds within a homeostatic range, preventing both excessive escalation (anxiety pattern) and excessive suppression (dissociative pattern). Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) are effective across anxiety, depression, and OCD—conditions that, in our framework, share the common feature of threshold dysregulation.

6. Clinical Applications

The PE-gating framework generates specific, testable predictions about neurological and psychiatric conditions as disorders of gating dynamics.

6.1 Epilepsy: Catastrophic Gate Failure + Re-Entrant Looping

Epileptic seizures represent the most dramatic form of gating failure. In our framework, a seizure occurs when GABAergic inhibition fails at one or more layers, causing gate thresholds θn to drop to near-zero. The result is a prediction error avalanche: signals cascade uncontrollably through the hierarchy, overwhelming each successive layer's processing capacity.

Critically, the cascade does not simply propagate upward and terminate—it re-enters. Higher layers, overwhelmed by unresolvable PE, generate their own prediction errors that propagate back downward, creating a positive feedback loop. This re-entrant loop is the formal equivalent of the hypersynchronous oscillatory activity observed electrophysiologically during seizures44,45. The seizure terminates only when metabolic exhaustion or residual inhibitory mechanisms restore sufficient gating to break the loop.

This model explains several features of epilepsy: (i) focal seizures begin at a specific layer/region where gating is weakest; (ii) generalization occurs via PE cascading to adjacent and higher layers; (iii) anticonvulsants work by reinforcing GABAergic gating (raising θn); (iv) the postictal state reflects metabolic exhaustion of the gating machinery; (v) seizure threshold varies with arousal, stress, and sleep—factors that modulate gate thresholds via neuromodulatory systems36,46.

6.2 Anxiety Disorders: Pathologically Low Thresholds

Anxiety disorders, in this framework, arise from chronically low gate thresholds across multiple layers. Normal interoceptive and exteroceptive prediction errors—which should resolve at L1–L4—escalate unnecessarily to L5–L8, engaging affective processing (L5), pattern-matching (L6), deliberative rumination (L7), and self-referential worry (L8).

Panic disorder exemplifies this pattern: normal cardiorespiratory prediction errors (L2–L3) escalate through lowered gates to trigger fear responses (L5), catastrophic interpretation (L7: "I'm having a heart attack"), and existential anxiety (L8: "I'm going to die")47. The PE that should have resolved at the organ level instead engages the entire hierarchy.

The mechanism is primarily noradrenergic: chronic LC-NA hyperactivation globally lowers θn. Treatment approaches that raise thresholds—benzodiazepines (GABAergic), SSRIs (serotonergic stabilization), beta-blockers (reducing peripheral PE generation)—are effective because they address different points in the gating cascade41,48.

6.3 Dissociation: Pathologically High Thresholds

Dissociative disorders represent the mirror image: gate thresholds are pathologically elevated, preventing normal escalation. Prediction errors that should reach conscious processing (L7–L8) are suppressed at lower layers. The phenomenological result is a disconnection from bodily experience (depersonalization), environment (derealization), or autobiographical identity (dissociative amnesia)49,50.

Dissociation can be understood as an extreme protective response: when PE magnitudes are chronically overwhelming (as in trauma), the system adaptively raises thresholds to prevent the catastrophic cascading seen in anxiety. However, this defense becomes pathological when it persists beyond the threatening context, producing the characteristic numbing and disconnection of dissociative disorders51.

Neuroimaging evidence supports this interpretation: dissociative patients show increased prefrontal inhibition of limbic regions during emotional processing52—consistent with elevated gate thresholds preventing L5 (affective) signals from reaching L7–L8 (conscious awareness).

6.4 Unified Clinical Spectrum

These three conditions—epilepsy, anxiety, and dissociation—map onto a single dimension of gating function:

Gate Failure (θ→0) ←——— Epilepsy — Anxiety — Normal — Dissociation ———→ Gate Over-suppression (θ→∞)

This spectrum predicts intermediate phenotypes: for instance, dissociative seizures (psychogenic non-epileptic seizures, PNES) may represent oscillation between the two extremes—periods of gate failure producing seizure-like episodes alternating with dissociative gate suppression53. The frequent comorbidity of anxiety and dissociative symptoms54 reflects dynamic fluctuation of gate thresholds within an unstable system.

7. Computational Implementation

7.1 Gated Recurrent Architectures as Primitive Implementations

The gating mechanisms described in this paper have direct computational analogs. Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks55 and Gated Recurrent Units (GRU)56 employ learned gate functions that control information flow through the network—deciding what to retain, what to forget, and what to output. These are, computationally, sigmoid-activated threshold gates operating on error signals.

However, LSTM/GRU gates operate at a single hierarchical level. The 9N framework suggests a hierarchical gating architecture in which multiple gated layers are stacked, with each layer's output gate feeding the next layer's input:

# Pseudocode: PE-Gated Hierarchical Processor class PEGatedLayer: def __init__(self, dim, threshold_init): self.generative_model = GenerativeModel(dim) self.theta = Parameter(threshold_init) # learnable threshold self.neuromod = NeuromodulatorInput(dim) # modulates theta def forward(self, input_pe): prediction = self.generative_model(input_pe) residual_pe = input_pe - prediction # PE after local processing effective_theta = self.theta + self.neuromod() # modulated threshold gate = sigmoid(|residual_pe| - effective_theta) output_pe = gate * residual_pe # gated escalation return output_pe, prediction class NineLayerHierarchy: def __init__(self): self.layers = [PEGatedLayer(dim=d, threshold_init=t) for d, t in layer_configs()] def forward(self, sensory_input): pe = sensory_input predictions = [] for layer in self.layers: pe, pred = layer(pe) predictions.append(pred) if pe.abs().max() < epsilon: # PE fully resolved break # energy economy: stop here return predictions

7.2 Advantages Over Flat Architectures

Standard deep learning architectures (transformers, deep MLPs) process all input through all layers regardless of complexity. The PE-gated hierarchy introduces adaptive computation depth: simple inputs are resolved by early layers, while complex inputs engage the full depth. This mirrors recent work on adaptive computation time57 and early-exit networks58, but with a principled theoretical motivation derived from FEP rather than ad hoc engineering choices.

Potential advantages include: (i) computational efficiency through early termination; (ii) interpretability—the layer at which processing terminates indicates the complexity class of the input; (iii) robustness—gate thresholds can be modulated to adjust the system's sensitivity-specificity tradeoff; (iv) biological plausibility—the architecture mirrors cortical processing hierarchies more faithfully than flat networks.

7.3 Training Regime

Training such a hierarchy requires a loss function that balances prediction accuracy against computational cost:

L = Σn [αn · Lpred(n) + β · C(n) · I(n)]
Eq. 5

where Lpred(n) is the prediction loss at layer n, C(n) is the computational cost of engaging layer n, I(n) is an indicator of whether layer n was activated, and αn, β are hyperparameters balancing accuracy and efficiency. This loss directly implements the FEP energy economy principle: the model is penalized for engaging higher layers unnecessarily.

8. Discussion

8.1 Testable Predictions

The PE-gating framework generates several experimentally testable predictions:

Prediction 1: Increasing GABAergic tone (e.g., via benzodiazepine administration) should raise the threshold for conscious detection of interoceptive signals, measurable via heartbeat detection tasks59. This follows from our model: enhanced GABA raises θn, requiring larger cardiac PE for escalation to conscious layers.

Prediction 2: Novel stimuli should produce measurably higher PE at early processing layers (indexed by early ERP components such as the mismatch negativity, MMN) that correlates with the depth of hierarchical processing engaged (indexed by late components such as the P300/P3b)60,61. The strength of the correlation should be modulated by global arousal state.

Prediction 3: Patients with dissociative disorders should show attenuated PE propagation from lower to higher cortical layers during emotional processing, measurable via dynamic causal modeling of fMRI data52,62. Conversely, patients with anxiety disorders should show enhanced PE propagation.

Prediction 4: Pre-seizure states in epilepsy should show progressive reduction in effective connectivity (gate function) between cortical layers, culminating in a cascade failure at seizure onset. This is testable via time-resolved connectivity analysis of intracranial EEG63.

8.2 Limitations

Several limitations of the current framework must be acknowledged. First, the mapping from nine conceptual layers to specific neural substrates remains approximate. While L4–L9 can be tentatively mapped to brainstem, subcortical, and progressively more anterior cortical regions, the precise neural implementation of each gate requires empirical characterization.

Second, the model currently emphasizes bottom-up (ascending) PE cascading but gives less attention to top-down modulation. In full predictive processing accounts, descending predictions actively shape lower-level processing6,7. A complete 9N-FEP integration must account for bidirectional gating.

Third, the threshold parameters θn are treated as primarily neuromodulator-controlled, but they likely also depend on structural factors (white matter connectivity, receptor density) that change on slower timescales. Long-term plasticity of gate thresholds—through experience, development, and neurodegeneration—is an important extension.

Fourth, the exponential cost function C(n) ∝ kn is a simplification. Actual metabolic costs of hierarchical processing are non-trivially distributed and may not follow a clean exponential. Empirical calibration is needed.

8.3 Relation to Other Frameworks

The PE-gating model is complementary to several existing frameworks. Global Workspace Theory (GWT)64 describes conscious access as information entering a global broadcast; our model specifies the gating condition for entry (PE exceeding the relevant threshold). Integrated Information Theory (IIT)65 quantifies consciousness via integrated information (Φ); our model adds a dynamic routing mechanism that determines which information is integrated. Higher-Order Theories66 describe consciousness as arising from higher-order representations; our framework specifies the PE-cascade process by which lower-order signals reach higher-order layers.

8.4 Implications for Artificial Consciousness

If the PE-gating hierarchy captures something fundamental about biological consciousness, it suggests that artificial systems implementing similar architectures may exhibit functional analogs of consciousness-like processing. This does not imply phenomenal consciousness in machines—the hard problem remains—but it suggests that functional properties such as adaptive attention, self-monitoring, and meta-cognitive control may emerge naturally in PE-gated hierarchical systems. This is a testable engineering hypothesis with practical implications for AI safety and alignment67.

9. Conclusion

We have proposed that the Free Energy Principle provides the mathematical framework for inter-layer gating in the 9-Neuron Theory of consciousness. The central mechanism is a threshold-gated prediction error cascade: PE at each layer is compared against a dynamically modulated threshold θn, with only unresolved PE propagating to higher layers. This framework:

(i) Provides an explicit routing mechanism for the 9N hierarchy grounded in established neuroscience theory;
(ii) Explains the energy economy of consciousness—why most processing resolves at lower layers;
(iii) Identifies neuromodulatory systems (GABA, dopamine, noradrenaline, serotonin) as dynamic gate-threshold controllers;
(iv) Generates a unified clinical spectrum from epilepsy (gate failure) through anxiety (low thresholds) to dissociation (high thresholds);
(v) Offers a computationally implementable architecture with advantages over flat processing networks.

The framework is testable, falsifiable, and integrates existing neuroscience findings into a coherent theoretical structure. Future work should focus on empirical calibration of gate thresholds, characterization of bidirectional gating, and computational implementation benchmarking.
