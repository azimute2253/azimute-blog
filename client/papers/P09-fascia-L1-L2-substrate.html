Fascia as the Anatomical Substrate of Layers 1–2: Rediscovering Meridians Through the 9-Neuron Framework

Luis Adures1,2 & Azimute2
1 Independent Researcher • 2 Kerberos Research Initiative
Correspondence: lk.adures@gmail.com
Submitted: February 2026 • Preprint • DOI: pending
Abstract
The 9-Neuron Framework proposes a layered architecture of intelligence from raw sensory input (L1) through metacognition (L9). Layers 1 and 2—corresponding to raw sensory detection and basic pattern recognition at the tissue level—require an anatomical substrate capable of whole-body signal transmission below the threshold of conscious neural processing. This paper argues that the fascial system constitutes precisely this substrate. We review evidence from Langevin's connective tissue hypothesis, Schleip's fascial mechanoreceptor research, Stecco's anatomical atlas, and the piezoelectric properties of collagen to demonstrate that fascia functions as a body-wide communication network operating at L1–L2 complexity. We reinterpret traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) meridians as empirical mappings of fascial planes documented over millennia. We critically evaluate Heine's neurovascular bundle (VNB) hypothesis—noting that while influential, only approximately 2% of claimed acupoint-VNB correspondences have been rigorously confirmed, not the 80% frequently cited. We conclude that fascia provides the physical infrastructure for L1–L2 processing and that the concept of "Qi" maps onto mechanotransduction and piezoelectric signaling through collagenous connective tissue.
Keywords: fascia, mechanotransduction, meridians, piezoelectricity, collagen, 9-Neuron Framework, connective tissue, acupuncture, L1-L2

1. Introduction

The 9-Neuron Framework (Adures & Azimute, 2026) proposes that intelligence emerges through nine hierarchical layers of predictive processing, from raw sensory detection (L1) through metacognitive self-awareness (L9). While upper layers map readily onto cortical structures—L7 to prefrontal reasoning, L8 to consciousness, L9 to metacognition—the lower layers present an anatomical puzzle. What physical substrate handles the lowest levels of signal detection and pattern recognition across the entire body?

Classical neuroscience has focused almost exclusively on neurons as the medium of information processing. Yet the body contains a continuous network of connective tissue—fascia—that envelops every organ, muscle, bone, nerve, and blood vessel without interruption (Schleip et al., 2012). This fascial web is not passive structural scaffolding: it is richly innervated, mechanically responsive, electrically active through piezoelectric and streaming potential mechanisms, and capable of transmitting mechanical signals across considerable distances (Langevin, 2006; Stecco et al., 2011).

We propose that the fascial system is the anatomical substrate of L1 and L2 in the 9-Neuron Framework. L1 corresponds to raw signal detection—the mechanical, thermal, and chemical perturbations sensed through fascial mechanoreceptors and free nerve endings. L2 corresponds to basic pattern recognition at the tissue level—the integration of these signals through fascial tensegrity and mechanotransduction pathways into coherent spatial patterns that feed into higher neural processing.

This reinterpretation carries profound implications. It suggests that traditional Chinese medicine's meridian system—far from being mystical or unscientific—represents a millennia-old empirical mapping of fascial communication planes. The concept of "Qi" (vital energy flowing through meridians) maps onto measurable biophysical phenomena: piezoelectric charge generation in stressed collagen, mechanotransduction cascades in fibroblasts, and interstitial fluid flow through fascial compartments.

2. The 9-Neuron Framework: L1 and L2 Defined

In the 9-Neuron Framework, each layer represents a qualitatively distinct level of information processing:

L1 and L2 are fundamentally pre-neural in the traditional sense. They operate below the threshold of spinal cord processing, at the level of tissue-embedded sensors and local signal integration. This is precisely where fascia operates—as a body-wide sensory organ and signal-integrating matrix.

3. Fascia as Body-Wide Communication Infrastructure

3.1 Anatomical Continuity

The fascial system is the only tissue system in the body that is truly continuous (Stecco, 2015). Unlike the nervous system (which has synaptic gaps), the vascular system (which has capillary endpoints), or the muscular system (which has discrete origin-insertion pairs), fascia forms an unbroken three-dimensional web from the superficial dermis to the deepest periosteum. Stecco's dissection studies demonstrated that fascial planes connect across traditional anatomical boundaries, creating continuous mechanical linkages from head to toe (Stecco et al., 2009).

Langevin and colleagues showed that subcutaneous connective tissue forms a network that can be mapped to acupuncture meridians, with acupuncture points corresponding to connective tissue convergence points where fascial planes intersect or change orientation (Langevin & Yandow, 2002). Using ultrasound imaging, they demonstrated that acupuncture needle manipulation causes measurable tissue displacement and fibroblast cytoskeletal remodeling up to several centimeters from the needle insertion point (Langevin et al., 2006).

3.2 Sensory Innervation

Far from being inert packing material, fascia is one of the most richly innervated tissues in the body. Schleip (2003) demonstrated that fascia contains Ruffini corpuscles, Pacinian corpuscles, Golgi tendon organ-like endings, and abundant free nerve endings (type III and IV afferents). The thoracolumbar fascia alone contains sensory nerve endings at densities comparable to skin (Tesarz et al., 2011). Taguchi and colleagues found that stimulation of fascial nociceptors produces pain responses qualitatively different from—and often more intense than—muscular or cutaneous stimulation (Taguchi et al., 2008).

This sensory richness makes fascia a primary L1 structure: it detects mechanical, thermal, and chemical changes across the entire body surface and depth, feeding raw sensory data into the processing hierarchy.

3.3 Mechanotransduction: L2 Pattern Recognition

The mechanism by which fascial signals become L2 patterns is mechanotransduction—the conversion of mechanical stimuli into cellular biochemical responses. Langevin (2006) showed that fibroblasts within connective tissue respond to mechanical strain by remodeling their cytoskeletons within minutes, changing their secretory profiles, and propagating signals to neighboring cells through gap junctions and paracrine signaling.

Ingber's tensegrity model (Ingber, 2003) provides the architectural framework: fascial tissue is organized as a tensegrity structure where local mechanical perturbations are distributed across the entire network through pre-stressed tension elements. This means a mechanical event at one location—a stretch, a pressure, an injury—creates a pattern of tension changes across a wide area, which is then "read" by the embedded mechanoreceptors. This is L2 processing: the transformation of point-source stimuli into spatial patterns.

3.4 Piezoelectricity: The Biophysics of "Qi"

Collagen, the primary structural protein of fascia, exhibits piezoelectric properties—it generates electrical charge when mechanically deformed (Fukada & Yasuda, 1957; Marino & Becker, 1970). This is not metaphor: crystallographic studies confirm that the triple-helix structure of collagen creates charge asymmetry under mechanical stress, producing measurable voltage gradients (Ahn & Grodzinsky, 2009).

Key proposition: The concept of "Qi" in traditional Chinese medicine—described as a vital energy that flows through meridians and can be influenced by mechanical intervention (acupuncture, acupressure, massage)—maps directly onto piezoelectric charge generation and propagation through the collagenous fascial network. Qi is not mystical energy; it is measurable bioelectric signaling through mechanically stressed connective tissue.

Oschman (2000) and Athenstaedt (1974) independently documented that the piezoelectric properties of collagen create body-wide electrical signaling pathways. When fascia is stretched, compressed, or twisted, the collagen fibers generate voltage differentials that propagate along fascial planes. These electrical signals can influence cell behavior, attract immune cells, stimulate fibroblast activity, and modulate nerve ending sensitivity—all without requiring neural transmission.

Additionally, streaming potentials—electrical currents generated by interstitial fluid flowing through charged collagen matrices—provide another mechanism for body-wide electrical communication through fascia (Grodzinsky, 1983). Together, piezoelectricity and streaming potentials create a continuous bioelectric communication system that operates independently of, but in parallel with, the neural system.

4. Meridians as Empirical Fascial Maps

4.1 The Connective Tissue Hypothesis

Langevin and Yandow (2002) performed the seminal study mapping acupuncture meridians to connective tissue planes. By digitizing acupuncture point locations from standard TCM atlases and comparing them to cross-sectional anatomy, they found that 80% of acupuncture points and 50% of meridian intersections corresponded to intermuscular or intramuscular connective tissue planes. This was a landmark finding because it provided the first plausible anatomical basis for the meridian system.

Subsequent work by Maurer and colleagues (2019) used high-resolution ultrasound to show that acupuncture needle insertion at classical points reaches fascial layers more consistently than at sham points. Langevin et al. (2001) demonstrated that the characteristic "needle grasp" sensation (De Qi) reported during acupuncture corresponds to measurable winding of connective tissue fibers around the rotating needle, creating a mechanical coupling between needle and fascial matrix.

4.2 Heine's Neurovascular Bundle Hypothesis: A Critical Evaluation

Heine (1995) proposed that acupuncture points correspond to locations where neurovascular bundles (VNBs)—clusters of arterioles, venules, and nerve fibers—perforate the superficial fascia. His initial report claimed that approximately 80% of classical acupuncture points corresponded to such VNB perforation sites, a finding that has been widely cited in the integrative medicine literature.

Critical note: While Heine's hypothesis is anatomically plausible and historically important, the 80% correspondence figure has not withstood rigorous replication. Subsequent anatomical studies using modern imaging and dissection techniques have confirmed VNB correspondence for only approximately 2% of tested acupuncture points with the specificity originally claimed (Fung, 2009; Lim et al., 2015). The original study suffered from methodological limitations including imprecise point location criteria, small sample sizes, and selection bias in the points examined. We include Heine's work for historical completeness but caution against citing the 80% figure as established fact.

This critical distinction is important for the 9-Neuron Framework. Our claim is not that acupuncture points are exclusively VNB perforation sites, but rather that the fascial network as a whole—including but not limited to VNB locations—constitutes the L1–L2 substrate. The meridian system maps fascial planes, not specific neurovascular puncta.

4.3 Fascial Planes and Myofascial Chains

Myers' (2001) Anatomy Trains concept, while developed independently of TCM, identified myofascial continuities—chains of muscle and fascia that transmit force and tension across body regions—that show striking correspondence with classical meridian pathways. The Superficial Back Line, for instance, runs from the plantar fascia through the gastrocnemius, hamstrings, sacrolumbar fascia, erector spinae, and epicranial fascia to the brow ridge—a continuous fascial chain remarkably similar to the Bladder Meridian of TCM (Dorsher, 2009).

Wilke and colleagues (2016) conducted a systematic review of myofascial force transmission and confirmed that fascial connections transmit mechanical forces between anatomically separate muscles, validating the concept of myofascial chains as functional units rather than isolated muscles.

5. Evidence Integration

5.1 Acupuncture Mechanisms Through an L1–L2 Lens

If fascia is the L1–L2 substrate, then acupuncture's mechanism becomes straightforward: needle insertion creates a mechanical perturbation (L1 detection) that propagates through the fascial tensegrity network as a pattern of tension changes (L2 pattern formation). The tissue responds through mechanotransduction cascades—fibroblast remodeling, collagen piezoelectric signaling, interstitial fluid redistribution—which collectively alter the L1–L2 signal landscape.

Langevin et al. (2006) demonstrated exactly this sequence: acupuncture needle rotation causes quantifiable fibroblast cytoskeletal remodeling, with cellular spreading and lamellipodia extension visible within 30 minutes. The mechanical signal propagates through the collagen matrix for distances of centimeters from the needle, far beyond what local neural stimulation would explain.

Goldman and colleagues (2010) showed that acupuncture increases local adenosine concentration—a purinergic signaling molecule released by mechanically stressed cells—providing a molecular mechanism for the analgesic effects traditionally attributed to Qi flow restoration.

5.2 Fascial Pathology as L1–L2 Dysfunction

Conditions involving fascial fibrosis, adhesion, or inflammation—such as plantar fasciitis, frozen shoulder, and myofascial pain syndrome—can be understood as L1–L2 processing dysfunction. When fascial tissue loses its normal viscoelastic properties through fibrosis or chronic inflammation, its capacity to transmit mechanical signals is degraded. The L1 sensors receive distorted input; L2 pattern recognition generates aberrant patterns; and higher layers receive corrupted ascending signals.

This explains the diffuse, poorly localized character of myofascial pain (Simons et al., 1999): it reflects disruption of a distributed processing network, not a point-source lesion. It also explains why manual therapies that restore fascial mobility—such as myofascial release, Rolfing, and instrument-assisted soft tissue mobilization—can produce improvements in conditions seemingly unrelated to the treatment site (Findley & Shalwala, 2013).

5.3 Fascia and Proprioception

The fascial system's role in proprioception further supports its L1–L2 function. van der Wal (2009) demonstrated that fascial tissue contains proprioceptive receptors at densities exceeding those in muscle spindles for certain body regions. The thoracolumbar fascia, in particular, is a primary proprioceptive organ for trunk position sense (Willard et al., 2012).

This means that body position awareness—a quintessential L1–L2 function (detecting body state and integrating it into a spatial map)—is substantially mediated by fascia rather than by muscle spindles alone. Schleip (2003) argued that the fascial system should be considered the body's largest sensory organ, surpassing skin in total receptor count when deep fascial innervation is included.

6. Discussion

6.1 Implications for the 9-Neuron Framework

Identifying fascia as the L1–L2 substrate has several implications for the broader framework. First, it establishes that the 9-layer hierarchy is not exclusively neural: the lowest layers operate through connective tissue mechanics and bioelectrics, with neural integration beginning at L2–L3 transition points where fascial mechanoreceptor signals enter the spinal cord and autonomic ganglia.

Second, it provides a mechanism for whole-body integration that operates faster than neural conduction for mechanical signals. Tensegrity-based force transmission through fascia occurs at the speed of sound in tissue (~1540 m/s in soft tissue), approximately 10-100 times faster than the fastest neural conduction velocities (Findley & Schleip, 2007). This means L1–L2 achieves a body-wide signal integration that is temporally prior to neural processing—exactly what the framework predicts for the lowest layers.

Third, it explains phenomena that have resisted purely neurological explanation: referred pain patterns, trigger point formation, and the therapeutic efficacy of manual therapies acting on remote body sites. These all involve L1–L2 fascial communication that bypasses or precedes neural processing.

6.2 The Status of "Qi"

We propose that "Qi" as described in TCM corresponds to a composite of measurable biophysical phenomena in fascia: (1) piezoelectric charge generation in mechanically stressed collagen; (2) streaming potentials from interstitial fluid flow; (3) mechanotransduction cascades in fibroblasts; and (4) purinergic signaling (ATP/adenosine release) from mechanically stimulated cells. The "flow" of Qi along meridians corresponds to the propagation of these signals along fascial planes.

This is not a reduction of Qi to mere physics—it is a recognition that TCM practitioners empirically discovered and systematically mapped a real biophysical communication system two millennia before Western science had the tools to measure it. The 9-Neuron Framework provides the theoretical context: Qi is L1–L2 information flow through the fascial substrate.

6.3 Limitations and Future Directions

Several limitations must be acknowledged. The piezoelectric properties of collagen, while well-documented in vitro, have been measured in vivo only under limited conditions; the magnitude and physiological relevance of these signals in living tissue requires further quantification (Ahn & Grodzinsky, 2009). The correspondence between meridians and fascial planes, while suggestive, is not exact—some meridian pathways do not map cleanly onto known fascial continuities (Dorsher, 2009). The critical reassessment of Heine's VNB data leaves the anatomical specificity of individual acupuncture points less certain than previously believed.

Future research should employ advanced fascial imaging (elastography, diffusion tensor imaging) to map fascial communication pathways in vivo and correlate them with traditional meridian charts. Direct measurement of piezoelectric and streaming potential signals during acupuncture would test the Qi-as-bioelectricity hypothesis. Computational modeling of fascial tensegrity networks could predict signal propagation patterns for comparison with empirical meridian maps.

7. Conclusion

The fascial system—continuous, richly innervated, mechanically responsive, and electrically active—constitutes the anatomical substrate of Layers 1 and 2 in the 9-Neuron Framework. Traditional Chinese medicine's meridian system represents an empirical mapping of fascial communication planes accumulated over two millennia of clinical observation. The concept of Qi corresponds to measurable biophysical phenomena: piezoelectricity, mechanotransduction, and interstitial fluid dynamics in collagenous connective tissue. Identifying this substrate grounds the lowest layers of the 9-Neuron Framework in verifiable anatomy and biophysics, while honoring the empirical wisdom of traditional medicine through a modern theoretical lens.
